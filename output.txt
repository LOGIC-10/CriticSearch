This paper develops a dynamic two-sided payments model with both heterogeneous households and merchants/firms to study: (1) The adoption of CBDC by households and firms, and (2) The impact of CBDC issuance on financial inclusion, informality, and disintermediation. Our model shows that there is a feedback loop where more households will adopt CBDC if more firms accept CBDC and vice versa -- incentivizing both households and firms will result in greater levels of take-up. Households are more likely to adopt CBDC if it is low cost, provides an attractive savings vehicle, reduces the cost of remittances, improves the efficiency of government payments, and (if accepted by merchants) offers a valuable means of payment. Firms are more likely to accept CBDC if fees are low, if there are tax exemptions or subsidies for transactions made in CBDC, and if households who prefer to make payments with CBDC make up a large share of revenue. Upon CBDC issuance, an economy can get stuck at a steady state with low CBDC adoption and small welfare gains if the features of CBDC which do not rely on merchant acceptance (remuneration, efficiency of cross border and government payments) are not sufficiently attractive, or if the households benefiting from these features make up a small share of merchant revenue. Temporary subsidies and using CBDC for government payments can spur initial take-up to transition an economy to a welfare improving steady state with high(er) CBDC usage. Greater adoption of CBDC will result in greater financial inclusion and formalization, but potentially the disintermediation of banks and card payments. Thus, there is a trade-off in designing CBDC for greater adoption. This paper develops a dynamic two-sided payments model with both heterogeneous households and merchants/firms to study: (1) The adoption of CBDC by households and firms, and (2) The impact of CBDC issuance on financial inclusion, informality, and disintermediation. Our model shows that there is a feedback loop where more households will adopt CBDC if more firms accept CBDC and vice versa -- incentivizing both households and firms will result in greater levels of take-up. Households are more likely to adopt CBDC if it is low cost, provides an attractive savings vehicle, reduces the cost of remittances, improves the efficiency of government payments, and (if accepted by merchants) offers a valuable means of payment. Firms are more likely to accept CBDC if fees are low, if there are tax exemptions or subsidies for transactions made in CBDC, and if households who prefer to make payments with CBDC make up a large share of revenue. Upon CBDC issuance, an economy can get stuck at a steady state with low CBDC adoption and small welfare gains if the features of CBDC which do not rely on merchant acceptance (remuneration, efficiency of cross border and government payments) are not sufficiently attractive, or if the households benefiting from these features make up a small share of merchant revenue. Temporary subsidies and using CBDC for government payments can spur initial take-up to transition an economy to a welfare improving steady state with high(er) CBDC usage. Greater adoption of CBDC will result in greater financial inclusion and formalization, but potentially the disintermediation of banks and card payments. Thus, there is a trade-off in designing CBDC for greater adoption. The introduction of Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) is expected to have significant implications for the traditional banking system and financial stability over the next five years. Here are the key impacts:

1. **Shifts in Banking Models:**
   - CBDCs could alter traditional banking models by allowing individuals and businesses to hold accounts directly with central banks, which may reduce reliance on commercial banks for basic services like deposits and payments. This shift could potentially diminish the role of commercial banks in the financial system, impacting their ability to generate profits and lend (BIS Finance, 2024).

2. **Funding and Disintermediation Challenges:**
   - A move towards CBDCs may lead to consumers transferring deposits from commercial banks to central banks. This could reduce the funding available to banks, affecting their ability to lend. Additionally, banks might face increased competition from fintech companies leveraging CBDCs to offer new financial services (IMF Working Paper, 2024; BIS Finance, 2024).

3. **Opportunities for Innovation:**
   - Despite these challenges, CBDCs present opportunities for banks to innovate. They can develop new digital services integrated with CBDCs, such as advanced payment solutions and digital asset management (BIS Finance, 2024).

4. **Financial Stability:**
   - The IMF highlights that CBDCs can introduce threats to financial stability, particularly during crisis times, where there could be an increased run risk. In steady states, if the central bank's balance sheet expands, it might lead to more costly or less stable funding for the banking system. To mitigate these risks, macroprudential policies and central bank lending expansions to commercial banks might be necessary. A key design consideration is to encourage the use of CBDCs as a means of payment rather than a store of value (IMF Working Paper, 2024).

5. **Monetary Policy and Financial Inclusion:**
   - CBDCs could enhance the control and flexibility of central banks over monetary policy by providing real-time transaction monitoring. They also offer potential improvements in financial inclusion, giving access to digital financial services to populations without traditional banking access (IMF Working Paper, 2024; Finance Magnates, 2024).

6. **Regulatory and Global Impacts:**
   - The rise of CBDCs is expected to fragment the global economy, creating divergent regulatory and technological standards. This could introduce inefficiencies and costs due to limited interoperability among different national CBDCs, which might only be interoperable among geopolitically friendly countries (Atlantic Council, 2023).

In summary, while CBDCs offer numerous advantages, including enhanced monetary policy implementation and financial inclusion, they also pose challenges to traditional banking models and financial stability. The balance of these effects will depend on the specific design and implementation strategies adopted by central banks worldwide.
